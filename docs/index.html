Numerical modelling of rifting

Sept 2022

Rifting is a geological process that occurs when a continent splits into two or more smaller landmasses. About 60 million years ago, a major rift occur at the North Atlantic, separating present-day Greenland, Canada, and Northwest Europe. Although the rift is geologically known, underlying mechanisms of rifting are still poorly understood. We made numerical models of multi-layer rock deformation and explored the effect of pre-existing mountain weak belt during the split of Greenland and Canada.

Lake basin stratigraphy analysis

Dec 2021

The Western Quebec Seismic Zone is one of the poorly understood regions due to the lack of instrumental earthquake records. Such records are vital for building code formulation and risk assessment. Since lake sediments were found to potentially record earthquake-related disturbance, a seismic reflection survey of the basin of Lac Simard, western Quebec was carried out. From the processed data, we identified two sediment sequences, one retaining lateglacial (~10-8 ka) records, and one postglacial, as well as acoustic facies that reflect disturbance of either tectonic origin, or likely due to meltwater influx when the entire region was covered by a huge, retreating Larentide Ice Sheet.

Capturing glacial landforms

July 2021

Mega-grooves are geological landforms left by glaciers and record information about the speed and direction of past glacier flows above the hard bedrock. As a glacier moves, it picks up subglacial material, which acts as an abrasive and can carve into the underlying bedrock. By examining these features, we can reconstruct the past glacial history of an area. Our study focused on measuring and documenting mega-grooves near Odessa, Ontario. The observed patterns reveal a southwest movement of the Ontario Lobe during the last glacial maximum, and the crossing striations suggest a northwest regression during deglaciation.
