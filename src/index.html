Rifting and formation of a continental fragment

Sept 2022

Rifting is a geological process that occurs when a continent splits apart and separates into two or more smaller landmasses. This extensional process sometimes generates a stranded terrance as a fragment of the larger continent, but the underlying mechanisms are not well-understood. Using numerical modelling, we investigated the influence of rock deformation due to an ancient mountain building event in the creation of Canada's Nain province, Labrador, Canada.

Exploring below the surface

Sept 2022

In my exploration geophysics course, we learned the interpretational procedures and field application of various geophysical survey methods, including EM waves, gravity, and Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR). We used the hammer seismic method, which involves striking a hammer to create an acoustic signal that penetrates the subsurface, undergoes reflection and refraction, and is picked up by geophones. Randon noise is cancelled out by stacking.

Lake basin stratigraphy analysis

Dec 2021

The Western Quebec Seismic Zone is one of the poorly understood regions due to the lack of instrumental earthquake records. Such records are vital for building code formulation and risk assessment. Since lake sediments were found to potentially record earthquake-related disturbance, a seismic reflection survey of the basin of Lac Simard, western Quebec was carried out. From the processed data, we identified two sediment sequences, one retaining lateglacial (~10-8 ka) records, and one postglacial, as well as acoustic facies that reflect disturbance of either tectonic origin, or likely due to meltwater influx when the entire region was covered by a huge, retreating Larentide Ice Sheet.

Capturing glacial landforms

July 2021

Mega-grooves are geological formations left by glaciers, which provide valuable information about the speed and direction of past ice flows on the bedrock. As a glacier moves, it picks up and carries subglacial material, which acts as an abrasive and can carve into the underlying bedrock. By examining these features, researchers can reconstruct the paleo-glacial dynamics of an area. Our study focused on measuring and documenting mega-grooves near Odessa, Ontario. Our analysis revealed a southwest movement of the Ontario Lobe during the last glacial maximum, as evidenced by the observed patterns. Additionally, the crossing striations suggest a northwest regression during deglaciation.
