Numerical Modelling

A recipe to create fragments of continents everywhere

When a new ocean opens, it can split a continental fragment from its parent plate. We conducted almost 100 geodynamic models of rifting on supercomputers, to test how fragments form and what factors influence that process. Using an automated data processing technique, we show that structural inheritance can directly control fragment width, while other factors like temperature can play an important role during the rift.

Ancient continental break-up modelled on supercomputers

About 60 million years ago, a major rift opened in the region that became the North Atlantic Ocean, between Greenland, Canada, and Europe. We still do not fully understand how a piece of Greenland's crust ended up on the Canadian side. We ran 3-D geodynamic rift models on supercomputers. These models tested how rock layers in a suture zone change shape during long-term continental extension.

Glacial Geology

Lake sediments reveal Ice Age clues or ancient quakes?

Canada's Western Quebec is an earthquake-prone region, but it has limited instrumental records for earthquake risk assessment. We analyzed seismic reflection profiles from an ancient glacial lake and identified sediments that record environmental change as the last ice sheet retreated. We also observed disturbed sediments that may be linked to ancient quakes or glacial melt influx.

Testing seismic surveying equipment in the Valley

Seismic surveying uses sound waves to study the underground. A common method is the hammer seismic method: a large hammer strikes a metal plate to create a signal, and geophones placed along a line record how the signal travels. By comparing when refracted and reflected waves arrive, we can estimate rock properties below the surface.

Mapping glacier movements through bedrock grooves

Glacial mega-grooves record crucial information about past glacial flow. We measured and documented these features in Odessa, Canada. Their patterns painted a picture of the region's glacial history: a southwestward advance of the Ontario Lobe during the last glacial cycle, followed by a northwestward retreat during the retreat of glaciers.
