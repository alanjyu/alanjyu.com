Geoydnamic modelling of rifting on supercomputers

Rifting is a process in which a continent splits into two or more smaller pieces. About 60 million years ago, a major rift occured in the North Atlantic that separated Greenland, Canada, and Europe. Why fragments of Greenland ended up in both Canada (the Nain Province) and Scotland (the Lewisian Complex) remains a geological mystery. Using numerical models, we simulated multiple layers of rock with varying properties under millions of years of extension and deformation. This allowed us to explore the impact of pre-existing mountain belts on rifting.

Sediment layers reveal geological history of Lac Simard

The Western Quebec Seismic Zone is known for earthquakes but lacks sufficient instrumental records and understanding of its underlying geology. By analyzing seismic reflection profiles from Lac Simard, Canada, we identified two distinct sediment sequences — one capturing lateglacial (~10-8 thousand years ago) records and another postglacial. We also found acoustic facies suggesting disturbances potentially linked to ancient tectonic activity or sudden surges of meltwater from the retreating Laurentide Ice Sheet, which once blanketed most of North America.

Reconstructing past ice flow patterns from glacial grooves

Glacial mega-grooves are etched into bedrock by the abrasive force of ancient ice sheets. These grooves reveal crucial information about the speed and direction of past glacial flow. Our study focused on documenting and measuring mega-grooves near Odessa, Canada. The remnant patterns paint a vivid picture of the region's glacial history: a SW advance of the Ontario Lobe during the Last Ice Age, followed by a NW retreat during deglaciation evidenced by crossing striations.
